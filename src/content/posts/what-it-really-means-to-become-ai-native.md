A lot of companies today talk about “adopting AI.” In practice, that often means layering a few tools onto existing workflows and hoping productivity improves.

That can absolutely create value. But I do not think it is the same thing as becoming AI-native.

To me, becoming AI-native is something deeper. It means rethinking how work is structured, how decisions are made, how people learn, and how humans and AI create leverage together. It is less about adding intelligence to the edges of the organization and more about redesigning the operating model around the reality that intelligence is becoming abundant, fast, and uneven.

There is already evidence that AI can produce meaningful gains. One recent analysis, published by the [World Economic Forum in collaboration with Distyl](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/01/next-decade-of-business-resemble-the-last-century/), notes that task-level productivity improvements often fall in the `15% to 40%` range. And in [Generative AI at Work](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161.pdf), Erik Brynjolfsson and his coauthors found a `14%` average productivity increase, with much larger gains for novice workers. That result matters because it suggests AI is not only a force for automation. It can also be a force for learning and capability building.

That is where I think the distinction matters.

An AI-native company does not just ask, “Where can we automate?” It asks, “How should this work change now that intelligence is broadly available?” That is a different question. It shifts the focus from tooling to workflow redesign. It forces teams to make context more explicit, define standards more clearly, and rethink where human judgment adds the most value.

It also changes how we think about people.

One of the clearest early signals of this shift is in software engineering. Recent data suggests that many developers already see AI not just as a productivity tool, but as something that is changing the shape of the role itself. More value moves toward problem framing, architecture, evaluation, and orchestration. In other words, the work becomes less about completing each step manually and more about shaping, guiding, and reviewing an increasingly intelligent system. That pattern is already visible in software, but I think it extends well beyond software.

This is why I believe becoming AI-native is also a learning challenge. The gap is not only technical. It is adaptive. People need to learn how to work with systems that are powerful but uneven. They need to know how to provide context, how to evaluate outputs critically, and how to intervene at the moments that matter. That is a different kind of capability than most organizations have historically built.

It also connects to a broader theme I have been circling here: AI readiness is not just about workflows. It is about the human skills, judgment, and learning loops that let organizations use AI well. In that sense, becoming AI-native builds on the same idea I explored in [AI readiness is not just about workflows](/posts/ai-readiness-is-not-just-about-workflows/): the advantage is not simply having access to better models, but helping people work with them effectively.

And it is also why the biggest payoff from AI may not come from cost cutting alone. The more interesting opportunity is growth, reinvention, and new business models. If leaders approach AI only as a way to remove labor, they will likely miss the bigger prize. The companies that pull ahead will be the ones that combine technology, workflow redesign, and workforce development in a way that compounds over time. Research on so-called [AI-native businesses](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/01/how-leaders-can-build-ai-native-businesses-to-capture-value/) points in that direction as well.

That, to me, is what it really means to become AI-native.

It is not a software purchase. It is not a pilot. It is not a prompt library.

It is a different way of organizing work, learning faster, and creating value in a world where intelligence is no longer scarce.
